How to Measure Shoe Size Accurately

Getting the right shoe size starts with one thing: an accurate foot measurement. Size labels like US 9, UK 8, or EU 42 mean different things in different countries. The only measurement that stays the same everywhere is length in centimeters (CM). That’s why we recommend measuring your foot in CM first, then using our CM to US shoe size converter or our full shoe size converter to get your size in any region.

Why CM Is the Best Starting Measurement

Centimeters are a single, universal standard. US, UK, EU, and Asian shoe sizes all use different number scales. A “42” in Europe is not the same as a “42” in Japan. But 27 centimeters is 27 centimeters everywhere. When you know your foot length in CM, you avoid mix-ups and wrong sizes. Shoe fitters and many international brands use CM as the baseline for this reason.

Detailed Measurement Steps at Home

You can measure your foot at home with a piece of paper, a pencil, and a ruler or tape measure. Follow these steps:

  1. Use a hard, flat floor. Do not measure on carpet; it compresses and gives a wrong length. Place a blank sheet of paper on the floor so one edge is flush against a wall.
  2. Stand on the paper. Stand with your heel firmly against the wall. Put your full weight on that foot so it’s the same as when you walk. Keep your leg straight.
  3. Mark the longest toe. With a pencil held straight up and down, make a mark at the tip of your longest toe (usually the big toe, sometimes the second). The mark should be right at the end of the toe.
  4. Measure in centimeters. Step off the paper. Use a ruler or tape measure to measure from the wall (or the edge of the paper that was at the wall) to your pencil mark. Write down the length in CM (e.g. 26.5 cm).
  5. Measure both feet. Repeat the steps for your other foot. Many people have one foot slightly longer. Always use the longer measurement when choosing a size.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Measuring in the morning. Feet swell during the day. Measure in the late afternoon or evening so your shoes still fit after a long day.
  • Measuring in bare feet when you’ll wear thick socks. Wear the type of socks you’ll wear with the shoes (e.g. athletic socks for sneakers) so the fit is right.
  • Using only one foot. Always measure both feet and use the longer length.
  • Pressing the pencil into the toe. Mark the tip of the toe without pressing down; pressing can shorten the measurement.

Standard Size Comparison Example

To see how one foot length translates across regions: a foot that measures 27 cm typically corresponds to US men’s 9, UK 8, and EU 42. The same 27 cm in women’s sizing is about a US 10. These are standard conversions; some brands may differ slightly. Use our shoe size converter for your exact measurement and gender.

Foot Width and Comfort

Length is the main factor, but width matters too. If you have wide feet, you may need to go up a half or full size even when the length is right. Many brands offer wide widths (e.g. W, 2E in the US). When you have your CM length, you can convert it and then check the brand’s width options.

Men’s, Women’s, and Kids’ Scales

Men’s, women’s, and kids’ sizes use different scales. The same CM length will give different US/UK/EU numbers for men vs women vs kids. Always choose the correct category (Men, Women, or Kids) in our shoe size converter so the result matches how the brand labels the shoe.

How to Measure Clothing Size Correctly

To buy clothes that fit—especially from international brands—you need reliable body measurements. This guide covers what to measure, what to use, and how to avoid common errors. Once you have your numbers, use our clothing size converter to see your equivalent size in US, UK, EU, and other regions.

Tools You Need

  • Soft measuring tape. A cloth or plastic tape that wraps around the body is best. Avoid a stiff metal ruler for chest, waist, and hips.
  • Flat surface and mirror (optional). Stand on a hard floor. A mirror helps you check that the tape is level and not twisted.
  • Light, close-fitting layers. Measure over undergarments or one thin layer. Bulky clothes will make your measurements too large.

How to Measure Properly: Posture and Clothing

Stand in a relaxed way: feet slightly apart, arms at your sides. Do not suck in your stomach or stand extra straight. Breathe normally when you take the measurement. The tape should sit snug against your body but not dig in. If the tape leaves a mark or feels tight, it’s too tight. If it slides down, it’s too loose.

Chest Measurement

For tops, jackets, and dresses, measure your chest at the fullest point. For most people this is at or just below nipple level. Wrap the tape around your chest, under your arms, and keep it parallel to the floor. The tape should be snug but not tight—you should be able to take a normal breath. Record the number in centimeters or inches.

Waist Measurement

Your natural waist is the narrowest part of your torso, usually 1–2 inches above your belly button. To find it, bend to the side; the crease that forms is your waist. Wrap the tape around your waist and keep it level. For pants that sit on your hips, also measure where you usually wear the waistband; that may be lower than your natural waist.

Hips Measurement

Stand with your feet together. Wrap the tape around the fullest part of your hips and bottom—often about 7–9 inches below your waist. Keep the tape parallel to the floor. This measurement is used for pants, skirts, and fitted dresses.

Inseam Measurement

Inseam is the length from your crotch to where you want the hem to sit (e.g. ankle or floor). Easiest method: take a pair of pants that fit the way you like and measure from the crotch seam to the bottom of the leg. Or have someone measure from your crotch to your ankle bone (or desired length) while you stand straight. Record in inches or centimeters.

Sleeve Length

For shirts and jackets, sleeve length helps get the right fit. Start at the center of the back of your neck, run the tape over your shoulder and down the outside of your arm to your wrist (or to where you want the cuff to sit). Keep your arm slightly bent. This gives a “center-back to cuff” measurement that many size charts use.

Differences for Men, Women, and Kids

Men’s sizing usually focuses on chest and waist; shirts may use neck and sleeve. Women’s sizing often uses bust, waist, and hip, and the same size number can mean different things in different countries. Kids’ sizes are often based on age or height (e.g. 110 cm, 120 cm). When you use our clothing size converter, choose the right category (Men, Women, Kids) and garment type (tops, pants, dresses) for accurate results.

Common Measurement Mistakes

  • Too tight tape: The tape should rest on your skin without compressing it. Indentations mean the measurement is too small.
  • Wrong posture: Stand normally. Sucking in or standing unnaturally leads to numbers that don’t match how you’ll wear the clothes.
  • Measuring over bulky clothing: Measure over one thin layer or undergarments only.
  • Tilted tape: Keep the tape level all the way around. A slant gives a larger measurement than your true size.
  • Guessing instead of measuring: Don’t assume your size in a new brand or region. Measure or use the brand’s size chart.

Why Size Labels Differ Across Brands

There is no single global standard. Brands use different fit models and design goals. Some use “vanity sizing” (smaller size labels for the same measurements). Others run true to size or small. After converting your measurements with our clothing size converter, always check the brand’s own size chart when you can.

Fit & Garment Guide

The same size label can fit very differently depending on whether the garment is slim, regular, or oversized. This guide explains what those terms mean and how to choose the right size when you shop across regions and brands.

What “Slim,” “Regular,” and “Oversized” Mean

  • Slim fit follows the body closely. There is 2–4 cm less room in the chest, waist, and hips than in a regular fit. Common in European and Asian brands. The result is a tailored, streamlined look.
  • Regular fit (often called “standard” or “classic”) is the middle ground: enough room to move comfortably without looking baggy. This is the most common fit in US and UK sizing.
  • Oversized fit is deliberately loose. Key measurements are often 4–8 cm larger than regular fit. Popular in streetwear and casual wear; the look is intentionally roomy.

How Fit Affects Perceived Size

Fit changes how a size feels, even when the label is the same:

  • Snug vs loose: A slim Medium will feel smaller than a regular Medium; an oversized Medium will feel larger. The “size” is the same; the cut is not.
  • When to size up: If you like a bit of room or have a broader build, go up in slim fits. In regular fit, use your usual size; if between sizes, round up for comfort.
  • When to size down: In oversized styles, you can often size down one or two for a less baggy look while still keeping the relaxed style.

When to Size Up or Down by Fit

  • Slim fit: Size up if you want more room or have broad shoulders or chest. Stick to your usual size only if you want a close fit.
  • Regular fit: Use your normal measurements. Between sizes? Round up for comfort, down for a neater look.
  • Oversized fit: Stay true to size for the full look, or size down for a less dramatic oversized effect.

Brand Differences: Some Brands Run Small or Large

Brands do not all use the same standards. Some run small (you may need a size up), others run large (you may need a size down). “Slim” at one brand can feel tighter than “regular” at another. Reasons include:

  • Different fit models and design goals
  • Different manufacturing and quality control
  • “Vanity sizing” (smaller size labels for the same measurements) in some markets
  • Different target customers (e.g. younger vs older, casual vs formal)

After using our clothing size converter, check the brand’s own size chart and, when possible, customer reviews about fit.

Why Sizes Differ Between US, EU, UK & Asia

Understanding why sizing systems differ helps you make better conversion decisions. These differences stem from historical standards, manufacturing practices, and cultural preferences.

Historical Sizing Systems

Each region developed its sizing system independently based on local manufacturing standards and average body measurements of their populations. The US system evolved from English measurements but diverged over time. The EU adopted metric-based sizing in the 1970s, creating a more standardized system. UK sizing maintained closer ties to historical English measurements, while Asian sizing systems developed separately based on different average body proportions.

Manufacturing Standards

Different regions have different manufacturing standards and quality control measures. European manufacturers often follow stricter sizing standards, leading to more consistent sizing across brands. US manufacturers have more flexibility, which can lead to greater variation. Asian manufacturers typically produce for smaller average body sizes, which is why their sizes run smaller than Western equivalents.

Vanity Sizing

Vanity sizing is the practice of labeling clothing with smaller sizes than the actual measurements would suggest. This is most common in US women's clothing, where a size 8 might actually measure like a traditional size 10. The practice is less common in men's clothing and European/Asian markets, where sizing tends to be more consistent with actual measurements.

Cultural Fit Preferences

Cultural preferences influence sizing. European and Asian markets often prefer closer-fitting, more tailored clothing, while US markets have historically favored more relaxed fits. This cultural difference affects not just the size labels but the actual cut and fit of the garments.

Metric vs Imperial Systems

The fundamental difference between metric (EU, Asia) and imperial (US, UK) measurement systems creates conversion challenges. While conversions exist, they're not always perfect because the underlying measurement standards differ. This is why centimeter-based measurements (CM) provide the most accurate conversion reference.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is EU shoe size 42 in US?

EU size 42 in men's shoes typically converts to US size 9. For women's shoes, EU 42 is approximately US size 10. However, this can vary slightly by brand. Use our shoe size converter for precise conversions based on your gender and specific measurements.

Why do sizes differ between regions?

Regions use different sizing systems because they developed at different times and for different markets. The US and UK grew from older English measures and then diverged. Europe moved to metric-based sizing, so EU numbers don’t match US or UK. Asia often uses its own scales and average body proportions. Manufacturing standards and “vanity sizing” (smaller size labels for the same measurements) also differ by country. So the same body or foot can get different size numbers in different regions.

How accurate is CM measurement?

CM (centimeter) measurement is the most accurate way to find your shoe size because it’s a direct physical measurement. Unlike US, UK, or EU size numbers—which use different scales—centimeters are the same everywhere. Once you know your foot length in CM, you can convert it reliably to any regional size with our CM to US shoe size converter or shoe size converter.

Is shoe size in CM more accurate?

Yes, measuring your foot in centimeters (CM) is the most accurate method for determining shoe size. CM measurements eliminate regional sizing variations and provide a universal reference point. When you know your foot length in CM, you can convert to any regional sizing system with confidence. This is why we recommend using CM measurements as your baseline.

Why does my size change by country?

Your size changes by country because each region uses different sizing systems with different scales and standards. A US size 9 shoe is equivalent to UK size 8 and EU size 42, but they're all the same physical foot length. Additionally, cultural fit preferences mean that even the same physical size might fit differently—Asian sizes often run smaller due to different average body proportions and fit preferences.

Are clothing sizes universal?

No, clothing sizes are not universal. While there are conversion charts between regions, sizing varies significantly between countries, brands, and even within the same brand's different product lines. A US size Medium might be equivalent to a UK size 12 or EU size 38, but the actual fit can still vary. Always check brand-specific size charts when available, as they provide the most accurate guidance.

How do I convert kids' sizes internationally?

Kids' size conversion follows similar principles to adult sizes but uses different scales. US kids' sizes typically start at size 1 and go up to around size 13, then transition to adult sizes. UK kids' sizes are generally one size smaller than US. EU kids' sizes use age-based or height-based measurements (like 110, 120, 130 cm). Use our converter with the "Kids" gender option selected for accurate conversions.

Why do shoe sizes have half sizes?

Half sizes provide a more precise fit, especially important for shoes where a small difference can significantly impact comfort. US and UK systems use half sizes (like 9.5), while EU sizing typically uses whole sizes only. If you're between whole sizes in EU, you may need to try both the size up and size down to find the best fit.

Why does my size change by brand?

Your size changes by brand because there's no universal sizing standard. Each brand uses different fit models, design philosophies, and manufacturing tolerances. Some brands employ "vanity sizing" (labeling items with smaller sizes than actual measurements), while others stick closer to standard measurements. This is why brand-specific size charts are so valuable—they account for each brand's unique sizing approach.

Can I trust online size conversion charts?

Size conversion charts provide a helpful starting point, but they're based on standard conventions that may not apply to every brand. We recommend using conversion charts as a guide, then checking the specific brand's size chart when available. Customer reviews can also provide valuable fit information. When in doubt, order multiple sizes if possible and return what doesn't fit.
